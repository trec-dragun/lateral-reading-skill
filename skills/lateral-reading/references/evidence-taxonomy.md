# Evidence Taxonomy

Use this ladder to decide which evidence deserves the most weight.

## Strongest Evidence

- Original item: the article, post, image, video, speech, interview, dataset, filing, paper, or document being discussed.
- Primary record: government page, court filing, regulator notice, bill text, company filing, official statistics, transcript, archival copy, or raw data.
- Direct expert or institutional source: named expert, relevant institution, original research group, or standards body speaking within its domain.

## Useful Supporting Evidence

- Independent reporting that adds original facts, interviews, documents, or on-scene observation.
- Reputable fact checks that show their work and cite primary sources.
- Context sources that clarify definitions, base rates, history, legal process, scientific consensus, or data limitations.

## Weak Evidence

- Aggregation that rewrites another outlet without new reporting.
- Anonymous claims without documents or corroboration.
- Social media screenshots without an original URL or archive.
- AI-generated pages, content farms, SEO summaries, and engagement bait.
- Opinion pieces used as factual support for claims outside the author's expertise.

## Independence Checks

Multiple sources are not independent if they:

- Reprint the same wire story.
- Repeat the same press release.
- Cite the same unverified social post.
- Depend on the same unnamed source.
- Quote each other without adding evidence.

When sources are not independent, describe the dependency instead of counting them as corroboration.
