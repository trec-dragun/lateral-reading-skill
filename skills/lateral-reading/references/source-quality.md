# Source Quality

Use this reference when the target source, author, outlet, or account affects trustworthiness.

## Source Questions

- Who created the target, and can that identity be verified?
- What genre is it: news, analysis, opinion, sponsored content, satire, aggregation, press release, or social post?
- When was it published or updated?
- Does it cite primary sources, documents, data, named experts, or only vague attributions?
- Does the source have relevant expertise, proximity, or access?
- Are conflicts of interest, political incentives, commercial incentives, or advocacy goals disclosed?
- Has the outlet corrected the story, changed the headline, or updated material facts?

## Common Red Flags

- Headline stronger than the evidence in the body.
- Vague sourcing such as "reports say" without links or named outlets.
- Old story recycled as current.
- Proposal, investigation, allegation, lawsuit, or arrest framed as proof or final outcome.
- Relative numbers without denominators or base rates.
- Money amounts without scale, budget share, time period, or comparison.
- Cherry-picked quotes that omit the surrounding statement.
- Cropped image, unsourced screenshot, or video clip without date and location.

## Calibrated Labels

Use labels carefully and explain the evidence behind them:

- `supported`: strong primary or independent evidence supports the core claim.
- `mostly_supported`: core claim is right, but framing or details need caveats.
- `mixed`: some important parts are supported and others are not.
- `misleading`: facts may be real but framing, omission, or implication distorts them.
- `unsupported`: available evidence does not support the claim.
- `false`: strong evidence contradicts the claim.
- `unverified`: evidence is insufficient or unavailable.

These labels can appear in natural-language `text` sentences; do not add a separate field unless the caller asks for one.
